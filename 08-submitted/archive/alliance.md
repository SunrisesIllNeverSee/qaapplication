### Answer

I began working on MO§ES™ in July 2025, which was also my first serious AI session. The earliest version of the idea came from trying to build an operator-based AI leaderboard: a way to measure what humans were actually doing inside AI sessions, not just what models scored on static benchmarks. That quickly exposed a deeper problem. To measure operator performance, I first had to identify what meaningful content survives transformation across prompts, summaries, and agent handoffs. That led to the Conservation Law of Commitment, the SIGSYSTEM measurement instrument, and eventually the broader MO§ES™ governance substrate. I chose this idea because the gap felt fundamental: AI systems can pass tests and still fail users because meaning drifts silently. I was drawn to it by a mix of necessity, obsession, and the belief that the trust layer underneath intelligent systems will matter more than the models themselves.

- from: alliance · 2026-05-26 · Q: "When did you start working on this startup? Why did you choose this idea?"

### Answer

There are strong companies adjacent to this problem, but they mostly sit in different layers. DevOps observability companies like Datadog and OpenTelemetry measure whether systems are running. ML observability companies like Arize and WhyLabs measure output quality against ground truth. Constitutional AI and policy-layer governance systems try to constrain behavior after the fact or at the model layer. What they generally do not measure is whether meaning or commitment survived transformation across an AI workflow. That is the category gap MO§ES™ is built around. I think we can succeed because we started from the signal itself rather than from policy or dashboards. The result is a system grounded in a falsifiable theory, protected by IP, validated by benchmarks, and already expressed across live product surfaces like Signomy, KASSA, and AQUA.
